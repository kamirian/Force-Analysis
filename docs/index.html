Why Average Error Is Not Enough

Current MLIP evaluations overwhelmingly rely on a single number — the MAE or RMSE of force magnitude. This is convenient but incomplete. A model with low average error can still fail in ways that matter enormously in practice:

The problem: A model can predict forces that point in completely wrong directions, or produce catastrophically large errors on a small fraction of atoms — and both failure modes are masked by the average.

These failures have real consequences. Static relaxations and NEB calculations typically require force convergence of 0.001–0.01 eV/Å. Misdirected or catastrophically large force predictions during molecular dynamics can destabilize trajectories and produce unphysical atomic dynamics.

This work addresses the gap by introducing two complementary metrics and analyzing force accuracy across three physically motivated regimes.

|ΔF|
Force magnitude error — eV/Å
How far off is the predicted force strength? Drives errors in bond lengths, lattice constants, and energy barriers.
|Δθ|
Force direction error — degrees
How wrong is the predicted force direction? A large angular error pushes atoms toward wrong energy minima even when the magnitude looks acceptable.

Three analysis regimes

DFT-substitutable

Highly accurate predictions

|ΔF| < 0.01–0.1 eV/Å and |Δθ| < 1–20°. Required for converged relaxations and reliable NEB barriers. For most current MLIPs, jointly satisfying both criteria is rare.

Catastrophic failures

Large-error atoms

|ΔF| > 1–10 eV/Å. Rarely reported by average metrics, but highly consequential — even a small fraction of these can crash an MD simulation or corrupt a trajectory.

Far-from-equilibrium

High-force configurations

|FDFT| > 1 eV/Å. About 26.8% of MatPES atoms fall here vs only 6.1% in MPtrj. Most benchmarks are dominated by near-equilibrium atoms and undertest this regime.

Key finding: The fraction of low-force-error atoms and the fraction of catastrophic-error atoms are more informative metrics than MAE/RMSE alone. The test dataset must include a substantial fraction of far-from-equilibrium atoms to meaningfully evaluate MLIP reliability for applications beyond near-equilibrium relaxation.

Analysis Code

The three Python modules contain all the reusable analysis and visualization logic imported by the notebooks. They are designed to be independent of any specific dataset path and operate on plain Python dicts with arrays of per-atom values.

matpes_frac_analysis.py

Core analysis engine

The computational backbone of all three analysis notebooks. Provides:

  • Fraction tables — fraction of atoms with |ΔF| below threshold, optionally joint with |Δθ| criterion
  • Regime panels — near-equilibrium vs. far-from-equilibrium error statistics
  • Conditioned MAE/RMSE — error metrics as a function of |FDFT| or |Δθ| thresholds
  • Catastrophic-failure analysis — fraction with |ΔF| above threshold and |FDFT| distribution of those atoms
  • Query utilities — find atoms/structures meeting any combination of |ΔF|, |Δθ|, |FDFT| criteria
  • Visualization — all heatmap and histogram types used in the notebooks
mlip_cdf_density_plots.py

CDF and density visualization

Handles all continuous distribution figures:

  • CDF computation — builds CDFs of |ΔF| and |Δθ| for all models, with optional near-eq / far-from-eq split
  • 2-D density panels — joint density heatmaps with marginal histograms for |ΔF|–|Δθ|, |ΔF|–FDFT, and |Δθ|–FDFT
  • CDF plots with inset — full CDF with automatically placed zoom inset for the high-accuracy tail or model-separation region
  • PCHIP-smoothed CDF curves, log-norm density colormaps, per-model color and linestyle support
heatmap_table.py

Heatmap drawing primitives

Low-level matplotlib code for the custom split-triangle heatmap style used throughout the paper — each cell is divided diagonally, with the lower triangle and upper triangle encoding two different statistics (e.g., |Δθ| < 1° vs < 20°, or MAE vs RMSE).

  • Cell-level: draw_triangular_cell, draw_rectangular_cell
  • Column-level: draw_triangular_column, draw_rectangular_column
  • Figure-level: triangular_heatmap_with_fraction_row, plot_fraction_panel
